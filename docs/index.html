3. Strategy

Our strategy was built on Buterez et al., "Transfer learning with graph neural networks for improved molecular property prediction in the multi-fidelity setting" (Nature Communications, 2024). In hindsight, reaching that paper was one of the biggest reasons the result landed where it did.

The auxiliary data

At first we built features and models from the pEC50 training labels alone. With only 4,140 pEC50 labels the accuracy plateaued quickly. The provided challenge dataset also carries a lower-cost PXR assay: a single-concentration reporter readout giving log2 fold-change (log2fc) at 8.25 µM and 33 µM, available for many more compounds. We knew this auxiliary signal was there but not how to use it well: simple ideas such as turning it into pseudo pEC50 labels only lowered accuracy.

The dataset is really a multi-stage assay funnel, and different measured labels reach different compounds. The dose-response gives both pEC50 and Emax (efficacy); a PXR-null counter assay adds a selectivity readout; and the single-concentration screen provides log2fc for far more compounds than are ever taken to a full curve. The single-concentration screen reports log2fc at two concentrations (8.25 µM and 33 µM), i.e. two measurements per compound. The grid below shows exactly which label each group carries. OpenADMET's own dataset-construction diagram, tracing the full screening funnel, is in the Phase 1 blog post.

Finding Buterez et al.

To break the plateau, we had Claude Code draft a research prompt that laid out the exact situation (scarce pEC50, an abundant auxiliary assay, pseudo-labeling failing) and asked for ways to exploit the auxiliary signal. Running that prompt through ChatGPT Deep Research surfaced Buterez et al.

The paper studies the multi-fidelity setting: when a few expensive high-fidelity measurements sit alongside many cheap low-fidelity ones, transfer learning with graph neural networks can lift the high-fidelity prediction, and using the low-fidelity signal as a representation or a prediction tends to beat using it as extra labels. Mapped onto Track 1:

Multi-fidelity conceptOur PXR correspondence
High-fidelity targetTrack 1 pEC50 (4,140 labels)
Low-fidelity measurementsingle-concentration log2fc at 8.25 and 33 µM
Low-fidelity model outputpredicted log2fc, appended to the features (main axis)
Low-fidelity representationfrozen embedding of an encoder pretrained on log2fc (diversity axis)
Downstream high-fidelity modelTabPFN, as a per-member readout on the frozen representation

This was exactly the fit we needed: it gave a principled way to put the auxiliary log2fc data to work for pEC50, instead of leaving it unused or misusing it as pseudo-labels.

Predicted log2fc (pred): the strongest feature

Following the paper, we trained a ChemProp model to predict the single-concentration log2fc and used that prediction, which we call pred, as a feature. Because the assay is measured at two concentrations, pred is also two columns (8.25 µM and 33 µM). How the ChemProp predictor is built is covered in the ChemProp model section below; here it is enough that pred became our single strongest input.

Figure 2 puts the four log2fc variants side by side against training pEC50. The predicted columns track pEC50 more tightly (Pearson r ≈ 0.75 and 0.80, over all 4,140 training compounds) than the raw observed measurements (r ≈ 0.72 and 0.50, and only over the 2,722 and 2,659 compounds where the assay was actually run). Predicting the assay, rather than using it raw, both fills in that coverage gap and smooths the signal.

It also does something the raw assay cannot. Because pred is computed from structure alone, it exists for every compound, including the blinded test, which carries no measured log2fc (in fact no measured label at all). The observed value simply is not available at test time, so only the predicted form can serve as a model feature there. This is quietly important: it is what lets an assay-derived signal reach the compounds we actually have to score.

pred does not stand alone. Figure 3 ranks a set of representative features by their single correlation with pEC50: pred sits at the top, ahead of the raw observed log2fc, the Boltz-2 affinity score (which points the opposite way, since it is a log10 IC50), logP, and the usual physicochemical descriptors, which are individually weak. In the model these weaker features still help, but as context around pred rather than as standalone predictors.

How far does pred alone get?

To size the signal, we fit a few deliberately simple models on the training pEC50 and scored them on the now-unblinded test (AS1 + AS2). The most telling comparison is not against our full system but against a familiar baseline: a default LightGBM on the full RDKit descriptor set.

4. The ensemble

The final system is not one model but an ensemble of nine members. They differ in how they turn a molecule into features, but share one thing: every member uses TabPFN as its readout head, in place of a trained MLP. So the members vary in their encoder or feature stack, not in the regressor sitting on top. Most encoders are frozen and pretrained on the auxiliary log2fc signal, in the multi-fidelity spirit of the strategy above.

Every member reads its representation with TabPFN. Only the two Boltz-2 trunk members carry no log2fc signal: they come purely from the predicted structure.

Members were compared with out-of-fold (OOF) predictions on a UMAP cluster split (chemical clusters kept whole across folds, so validation compounds are not near-neighbors of their training rows). We tried several splits, but the choice turned out not to matter much: none was decisively better on the leaderboard, so a UMAP split was fixed as a safe canonical and not over-tuned. The split is a tool for honest comparison here, not a result in itself.

The members are combined with Caruana forward selection (Caruana et al., ICML 2004), a greedy scheme that repeatedly adds the member that most reduces OOF error. Because the weights come from bagged selection counts, no single member can dominate, which keeps the ensemble from over-committing to one strong but correlated family.

It is worth asking how independent these members really are. Figure 5 shows the correlation between their predictions, and the answer is: not very. They are all highly correlated (r from 0.81 to 0.98, mean 0.88). The two tabular cores are near-duplicates (0.98), the two Boltz-2 members are too (0.97), and every chemistry member sits above 0.85 with the rest. So the ensemble is less about blending independent views and more a buffer: it keeps any one strong-but-correlated member, especially top500, from dominating the prediction. The most decorrelated members are the two Boltz-2 structural ones (0.81 to 0.88 against the chemistry members), which is exactly why they earn weight despite being weak on their own.

5. The members

The nine members fall into three groups. This section opens each one up, family by family, using the aliases from the roster above. First, how each member does on its own: out-of-fold on the training set versus the unblinded test (AS1 + AS2). The tabular pair leads; the gap between the two bars is the generalization story, and for most members the test bar is only slightly worse.

5.1 Tabular core (tabular-full, tabular-top500)

The two heaviest members share one feature stack: a wide ~2,103-dimension tabular matrix that concatenates several complementary blocks.

The two predicted log2fc columns are the same pred discussed in the Strategy section; they are also why these two members are the strongest single models.

CheMeleon

CheMeleon is not strong on its own (a TabPFN on CheMeleon alone scores ~0.512 MAE), but adding it to the stack clearly helped: the same tabular core went from MAE 0.443 without it to 0.421 with it. It contributes a learned molecular-graph prior that the hand-built 2D descriptors do not fully capture.

Boltz-2 features

The Boltz-2 block comes in two tiers. Tier-0 is Boltz's own scalar outputs: the affinity value, binding probability, and confidence scores (pTM, ipTM, ligand-ipTM, complex pLDDT). Tier-1 is our re-aggregation of Boltz's raw per-token confidence tensors (pLDDT, PAE, PDE) into pocket-level statistics, for example pde_pocket_ligand_min, one of the more useful structural features. A fuller breakdown of these Boltz tabular features may follow as an appendix.

top-500 selection

tabular-full feeds all 2,103 features straight into TabPFN. tabular-top500 first runs a per-fold LightGBM-gain top-500 selection and passes only those. Compressing the wide, partly noisy stack helps: out-of-fold error dips around K = 500 to 600 and rises again toward the full 2,103 dimensions (Figure 6). We fixed K = 500: MAE is essentially flat from 500 to 600, but 500 gave the best Spearman, so we let ranking break the tie.

Using LightGBM for the selection also buys a little interpretability: its gain says which features it leaned on. About three quarters of the gain goes to the two predicted-log2fc columns, and the rest is spread thinly across Mordred and CheMeleon (Figure 7). The selector is essentially "keep the two pred columns, then round out the space with descriptors."

Zooming in on the individual features tells the same story more sharply. The two pred columns are #1 and #2 by a wide margin (52% and 22% of gain); after them, no single feature reaches even 0.4%, and the tail is a mix of CheMeleon dimensions and interpretable descriptors like SLogP, qed, and the pose-Jazzy hydration terms.

Why both?

This is why we keep both tabular-full and tabular-top500. tabular-top500 is the sharp, compressed view (the strongest single model out-of-fold) and tabular-full is the broad, robust view of the same 2,103 features. Because top500 leans so hard on the pred/log2fc axis, running it alone is risky, so the ensemble holds both and Caruana splits the weight almost evenly (0.309 for top500, 0.288 for full). The caution was justified: an aggressive top-500 swap once regressed the public MAE from 0.407 to 0.413 while looking best out-of-fold.

Post-challenge aside: re-optimizing the member weights against the now-unblinded AS2 confirms the axis is real but easy to over-trust. Our top500 weight (0.31) was close to the AS2-optimal (0.33), and simply zeroing it would have edged us out by a hair. The failure mode is letting out-of-fold optimization inflate top500 to 0.84, which blows up on AS2, not top500 itself (issue #222).

5.2 Frozen embeddings (ChemProp, KERMT, MoLFormer, GatedGCN, AttentiveFP)

Five members share one recipe: pretrain an encoder on the auxiliary log2fc (two heads, 8.25 and 33 µM) over the 13,136 compounds, freeze it, extract one fixed-length vector per compound, and hand only that to TabPFN. The encoder is never fine-tuned on pEC50. The lesson, first proven on ChemProp, is that a frozen log2fc-pretrained embedding read by TabPFN beats both training the encoder on pEC50 from scratch and fine-tuning its head. (Encoders: ChemProp, KERMT, MoLFormer-c3.)

ChemProp

ChemProp is the prototype, and the strongest embedding member (OOF MAE 0.437, Caruana weight 0.151). It matters twice over: the same ChemProp-on-log2fc model is what produces the pred columns in the tabular core (Strategy and 5.1), and its 256-dimension frozen embedding is this standalone member. Two ways to inject the same log2fc signal, one as a sharp prediction, one as a spread-out representation.

The log2fc pretraining is a small multi-task setup:

  • Two regression heads, one per concentration: log2fc at 8.25 µM and at 33 µM.
  • Trained on all 13,136 compounds, with missing targets masked out.
  • Hyperparameters chosen with Optuna, tuned not for the log2fc loss itself but for the downstream pEC50 out-of-fold MAE.

Seed averaging was the main lever that sharpened pred: we pretrain ChemProp with several seeds and average their predicted log2fc, cutting variance. Going from 5 to 10 seeds improved the downstream (top-500 OOF MAE 0.399 to 0.397), and 15 seeds tapered off, so we stopped at a seed ensemble. That is why pred correlates as tightly with pEC50 as it does.

Freezing the encoder and reading its embedding with TabPFN, rather than fine-tuning it, is the recipe carried over from Buterez et al. (Strategy). The direct alternatives were all weaker out-of-fold: scratch pEC50 0.530, full fine-tune 0.507, frozen-head fine-tune 0.456, versus 0.437 for the frozen embedding. This is what made the frozen-embed recipe the template for every member below.

A curiosity worth flagging: those out-of-fold numbers oversold the frozen recipe's margin. Training ChemProp directly on pEC50 scored a weak ~0.53 out-of-fold, yet on the now-unblinded test (AS1 + AS2) it reaches ~0.48, much closer to the frozen-embed member's 0.44 on the same test. The frozen recipe still won, but out-of-fold ranking exaggerated by how much: another small case of OOF and the blinded test disagreeing.

Foundation encoders: KERMT & MoLFormer

The same recipe on two larger pretrained backbones. KERMT is a GROVER-style graph transformer (from NVIDIA and Merck, pretrained on ~11M ZINC/ChEMBL molecules) continued-pretrained on log2fc, giving a 3,200-dim embedding; it is the second embedding member (0.448, weight 0.111). MoLFormer is a SMILES transformer (DeepChem MoLFormer-c3), LoRA-pretrained on log2fc, giving a frozen 768-dim [CLS] vector (0.475, weight 0.040). Both predict about 0.92-correlated with the tabular core (same log2fc signal), but their different backbones shift the errors enough to earn weight. Not every backbone cleared the bar: ChemBERTa (the 5M/10M multitask-regression variants), run through the same frozen recipe, sat around 0.53 out-of-fold and failed the Caruana add-value gate (no MAE gain, negligible weight), so it was left out. A closer call was UniMol-v2, a 3D-conformer foundation model given the same frozen log2fc pretraining: it reached a respectable ~0.484 out-of-fold, near the members that stayed, but still did not earn enough added value for Caruana to keep it. Fine-tuning MoLFormer-XL straight to pEC50, by contrast, was weak (0.529) and dropped, echoing the ChemProp lesson.

Extra GNNs: GatedGCN & AttentiveFP

Two more GNN backbones (gated message passing and graph attention), same frozen recipe, 512-dim embeddings. Several GNN variants were tried; these two are simply the ones that came out best, and there is no deeper meaning to the specific architectures. Alone they are weak (0.474, 0.484) and carry tiny weights (0.018, 0.002). Their real job is the one the whole ensemble is built around: a buffer that keeps Caruana from leaning everything back onto the top500 / cheme-log2fc core. Drop them and it looks better out-of-fold, but family share climbs toward ~0.94, and that over-concentration regressed the leaderboard: shedding ~0.02 of member weight amplified into ~0.1 more on the core and cost ~0.006 MAE. Low weight did not mean safe to drop.

5.3 Boltz-2 structural (Boltz-pocket, Boltz-allpairs)

First, a clarification the name obscures: despite “structural”, these two never use Boltz-2's predicted 3D pose. They read Boltz's internal learned representation and hand it to TabPFN. Here “Boltz” labels where the features come from, not a docking or structure score. Read them as representation models that happen to be built on a structure predictor.

Both members come with the most winding backstory in the ensemble. The original plan was to use Boltz-2's predicted structure and its affinity head directly: co-fold PXR (UniProt O75469, 434 residues) with each ligand and read activity off the pose or the affinity score. Honestly, neither was usable on its own; the poses and the affinity scalar were too noisy to carry PXR activity (that affinity scalar survives only as one weak column inside the tabular core, back in the Strategy section). But co-folding every compound had taken about four days of compute, and the run had been launched with --write_embeddings, so the trunk representation was already sitting on disk. Rather than waste it, the question became whether Boltz is more useful as a representation than as a structure predictor.

So instead of the pose or the affinity head we take Boltz's internal trunk representation (the per-token single tensor s and the token-pair tensor z) and pool it into a fixed 1,024-dimension vector per compound for TabPFN. That vector is four blocks:

pocket
s_prot_mean384d
s_lig_mean384d
z_if_mean128d
z_if_max128d
allpairs
s_prot_mean384d
s_lig_mean384d
z_xp_mean128d
z_xp_max128d

Same s blocks (768d); only the z pooling mask changes.

  • s (single) is the per-token summary: s_prot_mean averages Boltz's single vector over the 434 PXR residue tokens, s_lig_mean over the ligand atom tokens. Each side described on its own.
  • z (pair) is the residue-by-atom interaction block: z_mean and z_max pool the protein-ligand pair cells of the trunk. This is where the pocket-ligand interaction lives, and it carries most of the signal.
  • pocket vs allpairs keep identical s blocks and differ only in which pair cells feed z: a fixed 13-residue core pocket (z_if) versus all 434 residues (z_xp), both by ligand atoms. In practice this barely moved the error.

How to pool was its own small search, over twenty variants of which tokens and which tensor to reduce.

Two things stand out. Which residues you pool over barely matters: a 13-residue core pocket and all 434 residues land within 0.001 MAE. How you reduce the pair tensor matters a lot: mean-pooling z is fine, but max-pooling z alone collapses to 0.576. The pair representation carries most of the signal (z alone ≈ 0.489; the single vectors are weaker), which matches Boltz-2's own affinity module emphasizing protein-ligand pairs: PXR activity needs the pocket-ligand interaction, not just the ligand's shape. Feeding the embeddings through an affinity-style head, or mixing in the distogram the way Boltz's native affinity module does, did not particularly help. Two poolings survived into the ensemble: Boltz-pocket (the 13-residue core) and Boltz-allpairs (all 434 residues), each about 0.486 alone.

The trunk dimensions have no interpretable names, so a non-linear reader helps: TabPFN (0.486) beat LightGBM and an MLP (0.512, 0.538) on the same vector. Each carries a small weight (0.046, 0.035), but together they add a genuinely different, structural axis to an ensemble otherwise built on 2D descriptors and log2fc. Dropping them or swapping in a re-pooled variant looked better out-of-fold yet regressed the leaderboard, the same reverse-amplification as the extra GNNs, so they stay as a structural reserve.

Step back and these two say something. They ended up the only members carrying no log2fc at all, and the most decorrelated from the rest of the ensemble: their agreement with the other members averages about 0.85, below the ~0.88 pack and among the lowest of any member, though still far from independent. Reaching roughly 0.486 from co-folded structure alone, with none of the log2fc signal that powers every other member, says the Boltz representation holds real standalone signal. Set against a direct language-model fine-tune like MoLFormer-XL, which only managed 0.529, it is a strong showing for a structure-derived model, and a sign of how much Boltz can do on its own. The caveat, echoing the ChemProp aside above, is not to overstate it: on the unblinded AS1 + AS2 a from-scratch ChemProp actually reached about 0.484, so structure is not uniquely winning here. But it is a nice negative-space finding in its own right: for this target, Boltz-2 was worth more read as a learned representation than used for its predicted structure or affinity.
